    LESSONS LEARNED
    		 TECHNICAL & SOFTWARE
    • Garbage In, Garbage Out: No algorithm—conventional or AI—can fix a poorly captured, blurry, or low-contrast image.
    • Prioritize Determinism First: Use fast, rule-based conventional vision (e.g., geometric matching, thresholding) for dimensions; reserve heavy AI models only for complex, organic surface defects.
    • Plan for Edge Cases: Factory floors introduce unexpected variables (e.g., vibrating parts, flipped components); code must include robust error handling and fail-safes.
    • Data Quality Beats Quantity: For AI inspection, a small dataset of perfectly labeled, highly diverse defect images is far more valuable than thousands of redundant images.
    		 HARDWARE
    • Lighting is 90% of the Battle: Proper illumination geometry (e.g., darkfield, coaxial) isolates the defect and minimizes ambient factory light interference, drastically simplifying software development.
    • Over-Spec for the Environment: Industrial environments are harsh; always use IP67+ enclosures, fanless cooling, and robust brackets to combat dust, heat, and heavy machinery vibration.
    • Match Resolution to Tolerance: Ensure your camera's spatial resolution (pixels per millimeter) leaves a safe margin (typically 3x to 10x) above the customer’s minimum defect tolerance..
    • Global Shutters are Mandatory: Never use rolling shutters for moving production lines; global shutters are critical to prevent motion blur and geometric distortion on high-speed belts.
    		 COMPANY
    • Scope Creep is Costly: Clearly define "what a defect is" in the contract; without exact mathematical or visual boundaries, clients will continuously expand inspection requirements.
    • Standardize to Scale: Reusing proven hardware architectures and modular software blocks across different projects cuts engineering hours and drastically lowers maintenance overhead.
    • Feasibility Testing Saves Margins: Always conduct initial lab testing with real samples before signing a contract to prove the defect is actually detectable under production constraints.
    • Documentation is the Product: Comprehensive bills of materials (BOMs), wiring diagrams, and user manuals reduce post-deployment support calls and ensure smooth factory handoffs.
    		 PEOPLE
    • Bridge the IT/OT Divide: Success requires close collaboration between the vision engineer (software/AI) and plant floor operators or PLC technicians (automation/hardware).
    • Manage Client Expectations Early: Operators often expect 100% perfect inspection; educate them early on false-positive versus false-negative trade-offs to build trust.
    • Cross-Functional Sync is Vital: Aligning early with procurement and vendors avoids costly supply chain delays and sudden component obsolescence during the build phase.
    • Value Floor Feedback: The machine operators know the quirks of the production line best; their operational insights are invaluable for tuning the system's user interface and alert logic.
